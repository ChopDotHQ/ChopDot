# VC Readiness Gaps And Plan

<discovery_plan>
- Evaluate ChopDot as if an investor is asking whether this is fundable
- Separate what is already strong from what is still too speculative
- Identify which gaps can be filled by sharper documentation and which require real-world proof
</discovery_plan>

## FACTS

- ChopDot now has a coherent product thesis:
  - shared commitment kernel
  - coordination first
  - proof second
  - no custody-first launch
- The repo now has current docs covering:
  - product direction
  - Teddy execution
  - architecture
  - API readiness
  - finance and monetization
  - launch perimeter and survivability
  - business flywheel / GTM map
- The current strongest wedge is:
  - group deposits
  - shared funding before execution
  - reliable closeout and closure

## INFERENCES

- ChopDot is now much more legible as a business.
- ChopDot is **not yet fully VC-ready** because the remaining gaps are no longer mostly narrative gaps.
- The biggest remaining gaps are:
  - proof
  - traction
  - retention
  - pricing validation
  - exact legal/operational posture

## ASSUMPTIONS

- “VC-ready” means a strong investor can understand:
  - what the product is
  - who pays
  - why it compounds
  - why it can defend itself
  - how it scales
  - what proof already exists
- This document is strategic guidance, not legal advice.

## 1. What Is Already Strong

## Product thesis

Strong:
- shared commitment is a stronger primitive than “expense app”
- the wedge is concrete
- the architecture direction is coherent

Why it matters:
- investors can now see the difference between ChopDot and generic expense tools

## Monetization logic

Strong:
- subscription-led today
- usage-priced trust actions later
- builder/API later
- no dependence on yield or custody spread

Why it matters:
- the money model fits the current product and legal posture

## Future extensibility

Strong:
- API-ready architecture
- modular rails
- future agent surfaces
- future builder/platform angle

Why it matters:
- the company can plausibly expand beyond the first wedge

## 2. The Real Gaps

## Gap A: Traction proof

Current weakness:
- there is no real traction story yet
- no demonstrated customer pull
- no proof that organizers or communities already depend on ChopDot

Why investors care:
- without this, the thesis is still mostly conceptual

What would close it:
- active test users
- repeated commitments
- completion rates
- early paid or committed pilots

## Gap B: Retention proof

Current weakness:
- the flywheel is plausible but not validated
- repeated organizer use is not proven

Why investors care:
- one-off use cases are weak businesses

What would close it:
- organizers creating 2+ commitments
- communities using ChopDot repeatedly
- proof that the product becomes a default workflow, not a one-time utility

## Gap C: Pricing proof

Current weakness:
- pricing is modeled, not validated

Why investors care:
- willingness-to-pay matters more than hypothetical ARR spreadsheets

What would close it:
- real pricing conversations
- pilot conversions
- evidence that specific ICPs accept monthly spend

## Gap D: GTM precision

Current weakness:
- ICPs are defined
- acquisition channels are outlined
- but the first repeatable distribution motion is not proven

Why investors care:
- they need to believe this can grow without founder-only heroics forever

What would close it:
- one clearly working acquisition path
- one segment that responds to outreach/content/referrals

## Gap E: Legal / operational precision

Current weakness:
- the posture is clear
- the exact operating setup is not yet fully pinned down

Why investors care:
- they want to know the company is not accidentally walking into custody/compliance risk

What would close it:
- explicit launch perimeter
- clearer jurisdiction and entity posture
- explicit “what we do / do not do” language

## Gap F: Metrics discipline

Current weakness:
- KPIs are mapped
- no operating dashboard or live scorecard exists yet

Why investors care:
- they need to see learning velocity and operating control

What would close it:
- one founder dashboard with:
  - commitments created
  - commitments completed
  - repeat organizer rate
  - conversion to paid
  - churn
  - runway

## 3. Gaps That Can Be Filled Now

These can be improved immediately with sharper packaging:

- tighter founder narrative
- tighter wedge narrative
- clearer product screenshots/demo flow
- cleaner current-vs-future architecture explanation
- clearer “why now” argument
- clearer ICP hierarchy
- clearer product roadmap sequencing

## 4. Gaps That Cannot Be Faked

These must come from reality, not documents:

- retention
- willingness-to-pay
- repeat usage
- conversion
- support burden
- operational load
- legal signoff confidence

## 5. What A VC Would Still Ask

1. Why does this win over Splitwise, Square, or manual coordination?
2. Who pays first, and why now?
3. Why is this recurring instead of one-off?
4. What is the wedge that turns into a platform?
5. What evidence shows this is not just an interesting concept?
6. What are the legal/compliance boundaries?
7. Why can this become big enough to matter?

## 6. What Needs To Be True For “Take My Money”

A serious investor will get much more interested when these become true:

1. the shared commitment loop works cleanly
2. at least one ICP repeatedly uses it
3. some of those users are willing to pay
4. there is evidence of repeat commitments
5. the legal posture is disciplined and credible
6. the product looks like a category seed, not a one-off feature app

## 7. The Best Immediate Plan

## Phase 1: Product proof

- Teddy restores the chapter / closeout loop
- founders validate the use cases and edge cases

## Phase 2: Early market proof

- recruit a small number of real organizers / communities
- run live commitments through the product
- track repeats and friction

## Phase 3: Pricing proof

- test organizer/community/provider willingness-to-pay
- validate what features trigger payment

## Phase 4: Investor packaging

- narrative deck
- metrics dashboard
- product demo
- traction memo
- legal posture memo

## 8. What I Would Put In Front Of A VC Later

Once the proof exists, the investor package should include:

- one-sentence thesis
- clear wedge
- product demo
- traction and retention metrics
- monetization proof
- flywheel explanation
- moat explanation
- API/platform expansion logic
- legal/compliance posture
- capital use plan

## 9. Recommendation

The next move is not “sell the fully complete story to VCs now.”

The next move is:

1. finish the current product correction
2. validate the kernel with real users
3. prove repeat usage and willingness-to-pay
4. then package ChopDot as a fundable business with evidence, not just coherence

## Best One-Line Summary

**ChopDot is now much closer to being investor-legible, but the remaining gaps are mostly proof gaps, not storytelling gaps; those must be closed with real usage, repeat behavior, and pricing evidence before the strongest investors will feel conviction.**
