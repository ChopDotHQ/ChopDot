## Why now

### 1. Coordination is fragmented

Groups plan together in:
- WhatsApp
- Telegram
- Signal
- email
- docs

But when money, readiness, or approvals matter, they still fall back to:
- one person fronting the money
- screenshots
- manual chasing
- weak social accounting

### 2. Existing categories do not fully solve the problem

- expense apps solve tracking after the fact
- booking tools solve provider scheduling
- wallets solve holding assets
- payment apps solve transfer

Very few tools solve:
- shared intent
- shared contribution
- shared approval
- shared closure

as one coherent object.

### 3. More groups and small operators now coordinate in temporary, recurring formations

Examples:
- popup communities
- retreat organizers
- workshop hosts
- collectives
- micro-teams

These groups are too structured for chats and too lightweight for heavy enterprise systems.

### 4. Future rails are improving, but trust semantics above the rails remain weak

Programmable rails, tokenization, and agents make coordination faster.
They do not automatically solve:
- who is in
- who approved
- whether something is actually ready
- whether a chapter is actually closed

That missing layer is where ChopDot can matter.

## Category creation

The category is not:
- expense splitting
- travel saving
- payment coordination
- lightweight escrow

The category is:

**shared commitment infrastructure**

or, more practically in the near term:

**shared commitment software**

## Why this category can win

It addresses a repeated, cross-domain job:

**turn a shared plan into an approved, funded next step**

That job appears in:
- trips
- communities
- events
- workshops
- service milestones
- embedded operator flows

## Why the wedge is credible

Group deposits are the cleanest first wedge because they combine:
- urgency
- real money
- multiple actors
- visible social risk
- a clear execution threshold

## Why this is not just a feature

If ChopDot gets the primitive right, the same kernel can power:
- deposits
- goals
- milestone releases
- expense closeout
- provider-side readiness flows

That is what makes it category-creating rather than feature-deep.

## What would invalidate the category

The category gets weaker if:
- the product collapses into generic expense tracking
- users only need it once
- the kernel cannot stretch across adjacent commitment flows
- the value remains cosmetic instead of trust-structural

## Best summary

The category exists because shared plans increasingly need structured, visible, approvable commitment state before execution, and current tools still split that problem across chats, trackers, providers, and payment rails.
