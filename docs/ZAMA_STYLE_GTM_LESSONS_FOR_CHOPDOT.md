## FACTS

- The Zama example highlights four strong GTM moves for technically complex products:
  1. simplify the category into a short memorable phrase
  2. define a category metric and own how the space gets measured
  3. create an incentive loop that turns education into community output
  4. make the launch event itself demonstrate the product
- ChopDot currently has stronger internal doctrine than public legibility.
- ChopDot already has the underlying ingredients for a stronger GTM surface:
  - a clearer product center
  - a clearer wedge
  - a clearer trust model
  - a clearer monetization posture
- ChopDot's current active doctrine remains:
  - shared commitment kernel
  - coordination first
  - proof second
  - chapter / closeout recovery first
  - subscription-led now
  - API-ready now, API-product later

## INFERENCES

- ChopDot does not mainly have a "marketing problem."
- ChopDot has a **translation problem**:
  - too accurate internally
  - not memorable enough externally
- The most useful lessons from Zama are:
  - make the product easy to name
  - make the product easy to measure
  - make the product easy to believe
- The least useful lesson would be trying to imitate token-sale theater or large incentive campaigns before ChopDot has a tight proof surface.

## ASSUMPTIONS

- ChopDot wants product-led GTM, not empty attention.
- The immediate goal is not mass-market hype.
- The immediate goal is clearer category framing, better proof, and better repeatable storytelling.

## The Four Lessons And How They Map To ChopDot

## 1. Simplify The Category

### What Zama did

It reduced a difficult technical stack into one short analogy that many audiences could repeat.

### What ChopDot should learn

ChopDot needs one short public frame that captures the product truth without forcing people to digest the full doctrine.

### Current problem

Internal wording is strong but too heavy for first contact:

- shared commitment kernel
- coordination first
- proof second
- deposits and closeout

All of that is directionally correct.
It is not yet sticky enough.

### Stronger public framing directions

Possible public frames:

- **commitment layer for groups**
- **shared commitments, not just shared expenses**
- **from "I'm in" to proven follow-through**
- **group coordination with visible commitment and closeout**
- **the layer between group intent and real follow-through**

### Recommendation

Use one short framing line publicly, while keeping the deeper doctrine behind it.

### Draft direction

**ChopDot is a commitment layer for groups.**

This is strong because it:

- expands beyond expense-app framing
- fits deposits and closeout
- fits organizer/operator value
- leaves room for proof and API surfaces later

## 2. Define A Category Metric

### What Zama did

It made the category easier to follow by naming a metric that grows only when the product is actually used.

### What ChopDot should learn

ChopDot needs one public metric that:

- reflects real product usage
- supports future dashboards and investor updates
- can later support ecosystem/partner conversations
- only becomes meaningful when ChopDot actually works

### What the metric must not be

Do not use:

- vanity signups
- generic MAU
- generic volume without context

Those do not tell the story.

### Best metric candidates

#### Public-facing category metric

- **Total Value Coordinated (TVC)**

Meaning:
- total value that moved through shared commitment workflows coordinated by ChopDot

Why it works:
- broad enough for deposits, closeout, and future extensions
- not dependent on chain-only usage
- understandable to investors, partners, and operators

#### Trust metric

- **Commitment Completion Rate (CCR)**

Meaning:
- percentage of active commitments that reach explicit closure

Why it works:
- directly tied to product truth
- exposes whether ChopDot actually improves follow-through

#### Operator metric

- **Organizer Risk Exposure (ORE)**

Meaning:
- value or burden still fronted, chased, or unresolved by the organizer

Why it works:
- directly ties to willingness-to-pay
- maps to organizer pain and retention

### Recommendation

Use:

- primary public metric: `TVC`
- secondary trust metric: `CCR`
- secondary operator metric: `ORE`

## 3. Build A Smarter Education Loop

### What Zama did

It used a creator/incentive program to turn product education into repeatable community output.

### What ChopDot should learn

The core lesson is useful.
The large-scale incentive program is not the right first move.

### What ChopDot should do now

Start with a **small trusted explainer loop**, not a broad creator contest.

Good early participants:

- real organizers
- pilot users
- community operators
- design partners
- a few credible ecosystem-aligned explainers

Good output types:

- use-case walkthroughs
- before/after organizer stories
- deposit or closeout examples
- short product demos
- simple visual explainers

### What ChopDot should avoid now

- large public leaderboard programs
- broad cash-per-post campaigns
- incentive structures that invite AI slop or sybil behavior

### Recommendation

Treat education as a **case-study loop**, not a creator-farm loop.

## 4. Make The Launch Or Demo Prove The Product

### What Zama did

Its launch event was also a live proof-of-concept for its underlying technology.

### What ChopDot should learn

The strongest marketing event is a real workflow that proves the kernel works.

### ChopDot equivalent

The equivalent is not a token sale.
It is one live, legible, real-world commitment flow that shows:

- shared intent becomes explicit
- state stays visible
- paid and confirmed stay distinct
- closure is honest
- organizer burden is reduced

### Best proof-event candidates

#### Option 1: Group deposit proof flow

Show:
- organizer opens commitment
- participants join
- deposits become visible
- replacements/dropouts are handled explicitly
- objective becomes funded or not

Best for:
- wedge clarity
- public commitment semantics

#### Option 2: Closeout proof flow

Show:
- chapter proposed
- legs created
- one leg marked paid
- receiver confirms
- chapter closes visibly

Best for:
- current implementation reality
- trust-state explanation

#### Option 3: Recurring organizer workflow

Show:
- same operator runs the process repeatedly
- ChopDot reduces chasing and ambiguity
- state history becomes an asset

Best for:
- monetization and retention story

### Recommendation

Use the **closeout proof flow first**, because it aligns best with the current implementation slice.

Then later use the **deposit proof flow** as the broader public wedge story.

## What This Means For ChopDot Right Now

## Current GTM gaps

### 1. Positioning gap

ChopDot is more coherent than its current public framing.

### 2. Measurement gap

The repo contains metric thinking, but not yet one clean public category metric.

### 3. Proof-event gap

ChopDot still needs one obvious demonstration that doubles as product proof and GTM story.

### 4. Content-loop gap

ChopDot has doctrine and research, but not yet a small repeatable case-study engine.

## Recommended ChopDot GTM Stack

## Layer 1: Public phrase

Use one short framing line:

**ChopDot is a commitment layer for groups.**

Supporting line:

**It helps groups move from vague intent to visible follow-through.**

## Layer 2: Public metrics

Primary:

- `TVC` = Total Value Coordinated

Secondary:

- `CCR` = Commitment Completion Rate
- `ORE` = Organizer Risk Exposure

## Layer 3: Proof event

Use one live closeout workflow as the first proof story:

1. chapter proposed
2. legs created
3. payer marks paid
4. receiver confirms
5. closure becomes visible

## Layer 4: Education loop

Use:

- case studies
- operator stories
- workflow explainers
- small demo artifacts

Do not use:

- broad creator farming
- generic paid posting

## What Not To Copy From Zama

- token-sale mechanics
- spectacle-first launch logic
- large incentive programs before moderation and proof surfaces exist
- category metrics that are too abstract or too easy to fake

## Decision

ChopDot should adopt the **structure** of the Zama GTM playbook, but not the surface form.

## Why

The reusable lesson is:

- simplify the thing
- measure the thing
- prove the thing

That is exactly what ChopDot needs.
