## Core rule

Early capital should buy:
- proof
- product reliability
- trust
- speed of validation

It should not mainly buy:
- premature scale
- over-hiring
- category theater

## Stage 1: Pre-seed / angel use of funds

Primary objective:
- prove the shared commitment wedge and retention path

Capital should go to:
- founder runway
- one to two core builders
- product hardening
- security/privacy hardening
- early design and UX clarity
- limited legal/compliance setup
- founder-led GTM and pilot validation

What should not dominate spend:
- large paid acquisition
- big sales org
- broad enterprise motion
- heavy protocol/token engineering

## Stage 2: Seed use of funds

Primary objective:
- convert wedge proof into repeatable operator growth

Capital should go to:
- stronger product and backend reliability
- provider/community workflow expansion
- pricing and conversion optimization
- security/process maturity
- support and operator tooling
- API-ready kernel maturation

## Suggested near-term raise logic

### Small pre-seed

Use when:
- the main goal is proving the wedge with minimal burn

Best for:
- founder-heavy execution
- validating retention and willingness-to-pay

### Realistic pre-seed / seed-prep

Use when:
- you want enough runway to build, validate, and sharpen ICPs without constant fundraising pressure

Best for:
- 18-24 months of disciplined iteration

## What investors should believe funds will unlock

1. working commitment kernel
2. real user validation
3. repeat usage evidence
4. pricing evidence
5. clearer ICP and acquisition motion
6. stronger legal and trust posture

## What not to promise too early

- massive consumer scale
- instant protocol/network effects
- full builder ecosystem
- tokenized platform expansion on a fixed timetable

## Best summary

Capital should be used to turn ChopDot from a coherent thesis into a validated, repeatable business loop, not to simulate scale before the wedge is proven.
