# Business Flywheel And GTM Map

<discovery_plan>
- Define the first credible ICPs for ChopDot
- Map how adoption, retention, monetization, and expansion should reinforce each other
- Identify the business loop ChopDot must prove before it can claim durable traction
</discovery_plan>

## FACTS

- ChopDot’s current product center is the shared commitment kernel.
- The strongest wedge remains:
  - group deposits
  - shared funding before execution
  - reliable chaptered closeout
- The current monetization posture is:
  - subscription-led today
  - usage-priced trust actions later
  - builder/API later
- The current launch posture is:
  - coordination first
  - proof second
  - no custody-first business

## INFERENCES

- ChopDot’s first business is not “everyone who shares money.”
- The strongest early buyers are people or operators with repeated coordination pain.
- The business will be strongest if the first ICPs are:
  - repeated
  - deadline-driven
  - socially risky
  - annoying enough that manual coordination is costly

## ASSUMPTIONS

- ChopDot does not need mass consumer scale first.
- It needs a narrow user base that has repeated commitment pain and will pay for relief.

## 1. First ICPs

## ICP 1: Organizer-led group commitments

Examples:
- retreat organizer
- trip lead
- event organizer
- community treasurer
- house organizer

Why this ICP matters:
- one person usually carries the stress
- multiple participants create coordination pain
- deadlines matter
- fronting the money is socially risky

Why they may pay:
- reduced chasing
- clearer who is in
- clearer who paid
- clearer when the group is actually ready

## ICP 2: Community / club operators

Examples:
- social club organizer
- community admin
- recurring event collective
- student or alumni organizer

Why this ICP matters:
- commitments repeat
- same operator pain keeps returning
- one workspace can hold multiple commitments

Why they may pay:
- recurring operational value
- repeated coordination relief
- stronger history and admin control

## ICP 3: SMB / provider-side shared funding workflows

Examples:
- workshop host
- retreat operator
- service provider with milestone release
- venue/event operator

Why this ICP matters:
- payment and commitment state block execution
- provider workflows are worth more than casual split tracking
- better closeout/release visibility is monetizable

Why they may pay:
- fewer failed commitments
- clearer customer readiness
- lower manual follow-up burden

## ICPs To Avoid First

- one-off casual split users with no repeat behavior
- large enterprise workflow first
- users who only want a free expense calculator
- users who mainly want custody, yield, or speculation

## 2. Acquisition Map

## Initial acquisition channels

### 1. Founder-led direct outreach

Best for:
- organizers
- communities
- early providers

Why:
- product still needs learning
- messaging still needs testing
- high-signal conversations matter more than broad awareness

### 2. Network and community seeding

Best for:
- popup communities
- group travel planners
- club operators
- creator-led gatherings

Why:
- these groups already coordinate in chats and DMs
- they feel the pain directly

### 3. Narrow use-case content and examples

Best for:
- proving specific wedges:
  - villa deposit
  - workshop deposit
  - retreat funding
  - expense closeout

Why:
- concrete examples convert better than abstract commitment language

## Acquisition channels to defer

- broad paid consumer acquisition
- large-scale performance marketing
- “build for everyone” marketplace motions

## 3. Retention Map

Retention will not come from novelty.
It must come from repeated coordination value.

## What creates retention

- repeated organizer use
- recurring community commitments
- provider-side repeated workflows
- durable history and role memory
- trust in closure and confirmation

## What weakens retention

- product only used once for a one-off trip
- no reason to come back after the first closeout
- weak difference from generic expense tools
- not enough operational value for organizers

## Retention rule

The business gets stronger when ChopDot becomes:
- the default place to prepare shared action
- the default place to close it out

## 4. Monetization Loop

## Loop

1. organizer starts a commitment
2. participants join because the flow is easier than manual coordination
3. clear progress / confirmation / closure reduces friction
4. organizer trusts the product more for the next commitment
5. repeated use justifies subscription
6. community or provider expands usage across more commitments

## What this means

Revenue expands when:
- one organizer runs more commitments
- one community adds more recurring workflows
- one provider embeds ChopDot deeper into operations

## 5. Expansion Loop

After the first wedge is proven:

1. more commitment types fit the same kernel
2. more recurring groups use the same workspace
3. providers adopt commitment workflows for real operations
4. builders want API or embedded access
5. ChopDot becomes infrastructure, not just an app

This is the long-term expansion path.

## 6. Candidate Flywheel

## Core flywheel

1. group coordination pain creates initial use
2. visible state + approval + closure creates trust
3. trust leads to repeated organizer use
4. repeated organizer use leads to paid plans
5. paid plans fund product reliability and stronger controls
6. stronger controls improve operator outcomes
7. better outcomes expand to communities and SMBs
8. broader repeated use makes builder/API layers more valuable
9. builder/provider adoption increases moat and switching cost

## Why this is not proven yet

- repeat usage is not proven
- retention is not proven
- willingness-to-pay is not proven
- expansion to provider or builder layers is not proven

So this is a **candidate flywheel**, not yet a validated one.

## 7. KPI Map

## Product proof KPIs

- commitments created
- commitments completed
- time from creation to closure
- confirmation completion rate
- repeated organizer usage

## Retention KPIs

- organizers with 2+ commitments
- communities with recurring monthly usage
- provider accounts with repeated workflows

## Monetization KPIs

- free to paid conversion
- revenue per organizer
- revenue per workspace
- churn by plan type
- retention by paid tier

## Business health KPIs

- CAC by early acquisition channel
- payback period
- burn multiple
- runway
- support burden per account

## 8. Business Risks

## Biggest strategic risks

- users see ChopDot as only an expense app
- weak repeat usage
- weak willingness-to-pay
- no clear acquisition channel
- product complexity grows before the wedge is proven
- legal/compliance posture gets dragged into custody-like behavior

## 9. What Has To Be True

For ChopDot to become a real business, these things must become true:

1. organizers use it more than once
2. communities use it repeatedly
3. at least one ICP will pay for trust and closure
4. the commitment kernel works across deposits and closeout without collapsing
5. support and operating burden remain manageable

## 10. Recommendation

The next business phase should be:

1. prove the chapter / closeout loop technically
2. validate group deposit and closeout use cases with real operators
3. test willingness-to-pay with organizers and communities
4. identify the first repeatable acquisition motion
5. only then treat the flywheel as real

## Best One-Line Summary

**ChopDot’s business flywheel is: solve repeated organizer commitment pain, turn that into trusted repeated use, convert repeated use into paid operator/workspace revenue, and then expand that same kernel into communities, providers, and later builders.**
